Leading a University Club: What I Learned in My First Year as President of GSEC

Dipankar Sarkar · January 2025 · 7 min read

When I was elected President of the Green Software Engineering Club (GSEC), I thought leadership meant having answers. I thought it meant standing at the front of a room, confident and decisive, with a clear plan for everything. I was wrong — and that wrongness taught me more than any textbook ever could.

The Weight of the Role

The title comes fast. The responsibilities come faster. Within the first week of taking charge, I was dealing with event logistics, member concerns, budget discussions with faculty advisors, and the quiet but real pressure of maintaining a club culture that had been built before me.

I quickly realized that leadership is not about the leader at all. It is entirely about the people around you. The moment I stopped asking "what should I do?" and started asking "what does my team need?" — everything shifted.

"A leader's job is not to do the work for others. It is to create the conditions in which others can do their best work."

Lesson 1: People Need to Feel Heard Before They Feel Led

In my first month, I made the classic mistake of proposing ideas before asking for input. I had a vision, and I was excited about it. But when I presented it to the team, the response was lukewarm — not because the ideas were bad, but because the team had not been part of building them.

I changed my approach completely. Before every major decision, I started holding open discussions. I created a group where members could drop ideas at any hour. I made it clear that my job was to facilitate, not dictate. Within weeks, the engagement improved dramatically. People showed up because they felt they owned a piece of what we were building.

Lesson 2: Consistency Matters More Than Passion

Passion is easy at the start. Every club president starts with fire. But passion without structure fades. I learned to build systems — regular meeting schedules, clear task assignments, follow-up processes. The members who initially seemed unmotivated turned out to simply need clarity. When they knew exactly what was expected of them and when, they delivered.

I also learned the value of showing up consistently — even when nothing significant was happening. Attending small events, checking in on junior members, being present in the group chat without always having an announcement. Consistency builds trust in a way that grand speeches never can.

Lesson 3: Your Ego Will Be Your Biggest Obstacle

There were moments when I was wrong and someone junior pointed it out. The instinct was to defend my position. Leadership, I discovered, requires the opposite instinct — to pause, consider, and, when appropriate, change course publicly.

Admitting a mistake openly did not make me look weak to the team. It made me look trustworthy. It showed that I cared more about getting it right than about being seen as right. That distinction, once understood, is one of the most powerful tools a leader has.

Lesson 4: Build People, Not Just Events

A club measures its success not in the number of events it runs, but in the growth of its members. I started investing time in identifying talented juniors and creating opportunities for them to step into visible roles — moderating sessions, leading workshops, representing the club externally.

That investment created something I did not expect: loyalty and momentum. When members feel their growth is tied to the club's growth, they stop being attendees and start being contributors.

Looking Forward

My first year has been a masterclass in humility, patience, and the quiet power of consistency. GSEC has grown — in events, in membership, in confidence. But more importantly, it has grown in identity. There is now a culture of initiative, of mutual respect, and of shared ownership.

If you are about to step into a leadership role for the first time — in a club, a team, or anywhere else — know this: the title is the smallest part of the job. The real work is in the listening, the showing up, and the daily decision to put your team before yourself.

That is what leadership actually looks like. And I am still learning it.
